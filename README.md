# steam-rpg-analysis
## Overview
That question changed everything. What began as a nostalgic conversation soon turned into a real plan.
The group decided to create the game they had always wanted to play, but dreams
cost money, and making money takes planning.

They had $70,000 and plenty of passion. The real question was whether that would be enough.

This analysis looks at the Steam games market to help them find an answer
and make informed decisions about the game they want to build.



A group of friends gathered to discuss their favorite childhood video games. What was meant to be a brief meeting turned into three hours of reminiscing about gameplay, music, pixel art, and especially the stories and characters.

At their next meeting, one friend brought his son. As one friend began sharing a story, the others, accustomed to his tales, only half-listened. Suddenly, the boy asked, 'What happened next?'

The friends agreed to meet on weekends to develop the story, lore, and characters. One friend, an artist, began sketching the characters, world, and monsters.

Another friend, a musician, played viola in high school and later learned piano and guitar, exploring genres including classical, jazz, rock, and R&B. He joked that flute and wind instruments were his sworn enemies.

The friend who proposed the idea expanded the lore. As a published writer of short stories and novellas, he drew on Greek and Japanese mythology, with inspiration from Disney, Miyazaki, and Star Wars.

The last friend was a programmer, though creating a video game had not yet been considered. The group continued to develop their world, sharing ideas and resolving disagreements. Eventually, the programmer suggested, "Why not make a video game? From the looks of it, I think this story would work better as a video game than a novel.”



The three friends considered the programmer’s suggestion. Although they had developed the idea, world, and lore, the writer was unsure how to present it. He was interested in continuing but lacked experience in game development or coding, as did the others. The programmer requested two or three weeks to prepare a presentation with examples from their favorite games and promised to provide data on their chances of success.



"We have a story we have developed together, but the question remains: how do we tell it? Do we step back and let you finish it alone, write your book, and move on? Or do we continue as a team, combining our creative input to turn it into something more?

I propose we move forward and turn this story into a game that will resonate with gamers for generations. We have all faced creative challenges, but we have always persevered. I am not suggesting a large-scale project, as we all have other commitments.

I recommend a small project with a $70,000 budget. I have collected data on profitable genres, potential language options, required exposure and visibility, and the figures needed to break even.

There will be no microtransactions. We have all expressed our views on this issue, and agree it is not fair to gamers, especially children.

In conclusion, I believe we should proceed and build this game. Kevin Eastman and Peter Laird began TMNT as a joke, only to expand and explore their idea as we have. Now it's a world phenomenon. I suggest we do the same."

This analysis is that presentation.
